BCI Safety – Concept Note

This Concept Note is an independent, descriptive framing of “BCI safety” for boards, regulators, risk leaders and ethics committees. It does not represent any official standard, authority or regulatory body, and it does not provide clinical, medical or legal advice. It simply outlines how the expression BCI Safety can be used as a neutral banner to structure governance, ethics and risk discussions around brain-computer interfaces (BCIs).

1. Definition

In this note, “BCI safety” refers to the set of safeguards, governance practices and oversight mechanisms designed to prevent or mitigate harms arising from the design, deployment and use of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs).

BCIs are technologies that record and/or stimulate neural activity to enable interaction between the brain and digital systems. They span invasive medical implants, non-invasive clinical systems and a growing ecosystem of consumer / wellness devices.

From a board and regulator perspective, BCI safety can be understood as the ability to:

  • Protect physical, psychological and cognitive integrity of individuals using BCIs.
  • Safeguard neural and cognitive data against misuse, re-identification or manipulation.
  • Ensure that AI components within BCIs behave in line with safety, robustness and fairness expectations.
  • Respect emerging notions of neurorights such as mental privacy, cognitive liberty and psychological continuity.

The term “BCI safety” is therefore not a clinical label, but a cross-cutting governance vocabulary that boards, supervisors and standard-setting bodies may adopt to organise their expectations and risk appetite around BCIs.

2. Context and drivers

Advances in AI-enabled neurotechnology and brain-data decoding are accelerating. Research prototypes demonstrate the ability to reconstruct speech, images or intentions from neural activity, raising profound questions around privacy and autonomy.

At the same time, BCI applications are moving:

  • from strictly clinical contexts (e.g. assistive communication for paralysis)
  • towards consumer, workplace and defence environments, often outside traditional medical / device regulation.

Public and regulatory attention is converging on the need for governance frameworks:

  • UNESCO and other international bodies are developing global ethical standards for neurotechnology, emphasising mental privacy, freedom of thought and protection against intrusive uses of neural data.
  • The OECD has issued a Recommendation on Responsible Innovation in Neurotechnology and a Neurotechnology Toolkit to guide governments on safety, autonomy and data protection.
  • The Council of Europe and UN human rights bodies are examining whether existing human rights protections are sufficient or whether new rights related to mental privacy and cognitive liberty are needed for neurotechnologies.
  • Several jurisdictions are starting to treat neural data as sensitive data and to enact specific protections in consumer and data-protection law.
  • Sector-specific regulators (for example medical device agencies) have begun to issue guidance on clinical BCI trials, non-clinical testing and safety risk control mechanisms.

In this evolving landscape, a clear, neutral expression such as “BCI Safety” can help boards, supervisors and alliances to align expectations, map initiatives and avoid fragmentation across multiple parallel terminologies.

3. Possible uses of this framing

The expression BCI Safety could be used as an umbrella phrase to organise different strands of work around BCIs. Without prescribing any specific model, this framing may help boards, regulators and expert communities to:

3.1. Governance and oversight

  • Structure board-level discussions on where BCIs are used or envisaged in the organisation, and what risk appetite is acceptable.
  • Define lines of accountability between clinical leadership, CIO/CTO, CISO, ethics committees and external partners.
  • Map how BCI projects connect with existing AI governance, cybersecurity, safety and data protection frameworks.

3.2. Risk and safety frameworks

  • Develop internal or sectoral risk taxonomies for BCIs (physical, psychological, data, AI, employment, discrimination).
  • Design safety by design checklists and pre-deployment assessments for BCI projects, including escalation paths for high-risk use cases.
  • Align BCI control plans with broader AI safety or neurotechnology governance charters adopted at national or international level.

3.3. Auditability, logs and incident learning

  • Specify expectations around logging, audit trails and monitoring for BCI-related systems, especially when AI models adapt over time.
  • Encourage the creation of near-miss and incident repositories to capture early signals of safety issues and support learning.
  • Clarify when and how independent audits or expert reviews should be triggered for high-impact deployments.

3.4. Multi-stakeholder dialogue

  • Convene multi-stakeholder fora (patients, workers, clinicians, engineers, ethicists, regulators, civil-society groups) under a shared “BCI Safety” heading.
  • Support the creation of public–private observatories or alliances focused on responsible development and use of BCIs.
  • Provide a recognisable label for communication with insurers, investors and rating agencies interested in systemic and liability risks.

In all cases, this note considers “BCI Safety” as a descriptive, optional vocabulary, not as a mandatory standard or a certification mark.

4. Limitations of this note

This Concept Note has strict limitations:

  • It is not an official standard, guideline or charter, and has no regulatory status.
  • It does not replace applicable law, medical-device regulations, human-rights instruments or data-protection rules.
  • It does not provide clinical protocols, medical guidance or professional practice recommendations for BCI research or treatment.
  • It does not address the full scientific and technical complexity of BCI design, testing and deployment.
  • It remains intentionally high-level and focuses on language and governance framing rather than implementation details.

Organisations considering BCIs should build their own frameworks drawing on specialist clinical, ethical, legal, human-rights and technical expertise and on the official guidance published by competent authorities.
